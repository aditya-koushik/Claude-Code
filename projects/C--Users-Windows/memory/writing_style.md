# Writing Style Patterns - Satya Nandala (Bit Foundry AI)

Extracted from "The Steam Engine Trap" blog post (final approved version, March 2026).

---

## VOICE & TONE

- **Direct, confrontational, no hedging.** Speaks to the reader as "you" and tells them what's coming whether they like it or not.
- **Confident authority.** States opinions as facts. "The System of Record is dead." Not "might be dying."
- **Conversational but weighty.** Reads like a smart founder talking to you over coffee, not a formal whitepaper.
- **No AI jargon.** No m-dashes. No emojis. No corporate fluff. No "leverage," "synergy," "ecosystem."
- **Personal conviction shows through.** "I visualize a huge growth..." - first person used sparingly but powerfully when stating a personal belief.

## SENTENCE STRUCTURE

- **Short declarative sentences dominate.** "So it stops using them." "AI breaks all three layers simultaneously."
- **Fragments used intentionally for punch.** "Slow and Unnecessary." "That's their moat."
- **Longer sentences used for escalation/narrative flow.** When building an argument step by step, sentences chain together with natural connectors.
- **Questions used as transitions.** "If SaaS tools lose value, what gains?" - leads into the answer immediately.
- **Repetition as a rhetorical device.** "They don't own the data. They don't own the applications. They don't own the IP. They own the coordination."

## PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE

- **One idea per paragraph.** Most paragraphs are 1-3 sentences.
- **Single-line paragraphs for impact.** "And you are about to make the same mistake." stands alone.
- **No transition filler.** Sections don't start with "Now let's talk about..." Just states the next point.
- **"But here's..." as a pivot.** Used to introduce counterintuitive nuances: "But here's what happens next." "But here's the nuance most people miss."

## ARGUMENT STRUCTURE

- **Historical analogy opens.** Start with a well-known historical parallel (steam engine, electric motors) then map it to the present.
- **Layered escalation.** Build the argument step by step. Phase 1 > Phase 2 > Phase 3. The reader follows the logical chain.
- **Concrete examples over theory.** Names real companies (Infosys, Salesforce, Square). Uses real numbers (4,000 out of 10,000 people).
- **Binary close.** Ends with two clear options: Option A (safe, dying) vs Option B (hard, winning). Forces the reader to choose.
- **Rhetorical question ending.** Final lines are questions that linger. "Will you be the one bolting steam engines onto water wheels? Or will you be the one redesigning the factory?"

## FORMATTING PREFERENCES

- **Bold for key terms and section labels.** Layer names, year phases, option labels.
- **Quoted phrases for coined concepts.** "System of Record," "Consultative edge," "System of Intelligence."
- **Horizontal rules (---) between major sections** but not between every sub-section.
- **Bullet format for short parallel lists** (3-4 items). E.g. "They don't own the data / applications / IP." But no bullets in flowing prose.
- **Capitalization for emphasis on key terms.** "The Orchestration Layer." "Consultative edge" with capital C.
- **Equals signs for visual chains.** "Four tools = Four logins = Four invoices." Used instead of periods when showing cause-effect equivalence.

## LINKEDIN POST FORMATTING

- **Post title goes in body text as the first line**, not in the heading/label. The hook IS the title.
- **Personal conviction closing lines.** Ends posts with a personal observation. E.g. "I could sense an opportunity for consulting industry to make a come back."
- **Shock first, reframe second.** Lead with the provocative claim, then offer the nuanced take. Not the other way around.
- **Urgency uses exclamation marks sparingly but deliberately.** "Not in the next 5 years. Now!" - only when the point demands it.
- **Prefers TCS over Cognizant** when listing Indian IT giants (Infosys, TCS, Wipro, Accenture).

## CONTENT PRINCIPLES

- **Enterprise/business lens.** Always ties back to how this affects real companies, real revenue, real jobs.
- **Not afraid of bold predictions.** "SaaS companies lose 60-80% of their enterprise customers."
- **Names names.** Doesn't hide behind "some companies" - says Infosys, Cognizant, Wipro, Accenture, Jack Dorsey, Elon Musk.
- **Solution-oriented.** Doesn't just diagnose the problem. Points to the way forward (orchestration, consultative edge, entrepreneurship).
- **Empathy for the displaced.** Acknowledges the employment problem honestly. Doesn't pretend AI solves everything.

## WHAT TO AVOID

- M-dashes (use commas or periods instead)
- Emojis
- AI/tech jargon ("leverage," "ecosystem," "paradigm shift," "disrupt")
- Hedging language ("might," "could potentially," "it's possible that")
- Filler transitions ("In conclusion," "Moving on," "Let's now discuss")
- Over-explaining obvious points
- Academic/formal tone
- Passive voice
