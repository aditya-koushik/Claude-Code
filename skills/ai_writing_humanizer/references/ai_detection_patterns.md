# AI Detection Patterns

## 15 Patterns That Make Text Sound AI-Generated

Each pattern includes what to look for, an AI-sounding example, and the human fix. If you find any of these in content, rewrite immediately.

---

### Pattern 1: Em-Dashes for Parenthetical Asides

**What it looks like:** Using em-dashes to insert clarifying phrases mid-sentence. ChatGPT does this constantly.

**AI version:**
> The platform -- which was built for enterprise teams -- processes invoices 10x faster than manual entry.

**Human fix:**
> The platform processes invoices 10x faster than manual entry. We built it for enterprise teams.

**Rule:** Zero em-dashes. Use periods. Use commas. Use parentheses if you must. Never em-dashes.

---

### Pattern 2: "Delve Into"

**What it looks like:** Using "delve" as a verb. This is the single most common AI tell-word. No human uses "delve" in casual business writing.

**AI version:**
> Let's delve into the three key factors driving enterprise AI adoption.

**Human fix:**
> Here are the three things driving enterprise AI adoption.

**Rule:** "Delve" is banned. Always. Alternatives: dig into, look at, break down, examine, explore.

---

### Pattern 3: "It's Important to Note"

**What it looks like:** Prefacing a point with unnecessary hedging that adds zero information.

**AI version:**
> It's important to note that most AI implementations fail in the first year.

**Human fix:**
> Most AI implementations fail in the first year.

**Rule:** Cut the prefix entirely. State the point directly. If it is important, the reader will know. You do not need to announce it.

---

### Pattern 4: "In Today's Rapidly Evolving"

**What it looks like:** Opening with a vague reference to how fast things are changing. This is the most generic opener in AI-generated content.

**AI version:**
> In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape, enterprises must adapt their AI strategies to remain competitive.

**Human fix:**
> Enterprises that do not have an AI strategy by Q3 will lose to competitors that do.

**Rule:** Never open with "in today's" anything. Start with a specific claim, number, or story.

---

### Pattern 5: List-Heavy Structure

**What it looks like:** Every section contains a bulleted list. AI defaults to lists because they are easy to generate. Human writers mix prose, lists, and examples.

**AI version:**
> Key benefits include:
> - Improved efficiency
> - Reduced costs
> - Better customer satisfaction
> - Enhanced scalability
> - Streamlined operations

**Human fix:**
> We cut processing time by 82%. That saved $340,000 in year one. Not from the software. From redeploying the two people who used to do data entry.

**Rule:** Replace generic lists with specific stories. One concrete example beats five bullet points.

---

### Pattern 6: Perfectly Balanced Paragraphs

**What it looks like:** Every paragraph is exactly 3-4 sentences. Same length. Same structure. Humans do not write with this mechanical regularity.

**AI version:**
> [3-sentence paragraph about problem]
> [3-sentence paragraph about solution]
> [3-sentence paragraph about benefit]
> [3-sentence paragraph about next steps]

**Human fix:** Vary paragraph length. One sentence. Then three. Then one again. Then two. The rhythm creates energy. Mechanical sameness creates boredom.

**Rule:** Use 1/3/1 rhythm intentionally. Break the pattern when it serves the point.

---

### Pattern 7: Hedge Words

**What it looks like:** Softening every claim with qualifiers. AI models are trained to avoid strong assertions.

**AI version:**
> This could potentially help organizations somewhat improve their operational efficiency in certain scenarios.

**Human fix:**
> This cuts operational costs by 40%.

**Rule:** Banned hedge words: might, perhaps, possibly, potentially, arguably, somewhat, in some cases, to a certain extent. Either commit to the claim or cut it.

---

### Pattern 8: "Moreover/Furthermore/Additionally"

**What it looks like:** Using formal transition words between paragraphs. These words add no meaning. They are filler.

**AI version:**
> The platform reduces processing time by 80%. Furthermore, it eliminates manual data entry. Additionally, it integrates with existing systems. Moreover, it provides real-time analytics.

**Human fix:**
> The platform cuts processing time by 80%. It kills manual data entry. It works with your existing systems. And it shows you what is happening in real time.

**Rule:** Cut "moreover," "furthermore," "additionally," "in addition," and "in conclusion." Start the next sentence directly. If the connection is not obvious without a transition word, the writing is broken.

---

### Pattern 9: "Leverage" as a Verb

**What it looks like:** Using "leverage" when "use" works fine. AI loves this word. Humans rarely say it in conversation.

**AI version:**
> Organizations can leverage AI to unlock new revenue streams and drive transformative outcomes.

**Human fix:**
> Use AI to find revenue you are leaving on the table.

**Rule:** "Leverage" is banned when used as a verb. Alternatives: use, apply, deploy, put to work.

---

### Pattern 10: Symmetrical Sentence Pairs

**What it looks like:** Two sentences with mirror structures back to back. AI generates these because they are syntactically clean. They sound robotic.

**AI version:**
> The old way was slow and expensive. The new way is fast and affordable.

**Human fix:**
> The old way took 14 days and cost $200 per invoice. Now it takes 3 hours. The cost dropped 90%.

**Rule:** Break the symmetry with specific numbers and uneven sentence lengths.

---

### Pattern 11: "The Landscape"

**What it looks like:** Referring to a market or industry as a "landscape." AI models use this metaphor compulsively.

**AI version:**
> The enterprise AI landscape is shifting dramatically as new players enter the market.

**Human fix:**
> Three new AI companies raised $100M+ this quarter. The incumbents are scrambling.

**Rule:** "Landscape" is banned. Alternatives: market, space, industry. Or skip the abstraction and describe what is actually happening.

---

### Pattern 12: Overuse of "Comprehensive"

**What it looks like:** Describing anything as "comprehensive." AI uses this to signal completeness without saying anything specific.

**AI version:**
> Our comprehensive solution provides a comprehensive approach to comprehensive data management.

**Human fix:**
> The platform handles ingestion, storage, processing, and analytics. One tool. No integrations needed.

**Rule:** "Comprehensive" is banned. Instead, list the specific things that make it complete.

---

### Pattern 13: The "Not Only... But Also" Construction

**What it looks like:** A grammatical structure that AI loves and humans rarely use in informal writing.

**AI version:**
> Our platform not only reduces costs but also improves accuracy while simultaneously enhancing team productivity.

**Human fix:**
> Costs drop. Accuracy goes up. Your team gets 4 hours back per day.

**Rule:** Replace "not only... but also" with short, direct sentences. One benefit per sentence.

---

### Pattern 14: Vague Superlatives

**What it looks like:** Using words like "revolutionary," "groundbreaking," "cutting-edge," or "game-changing" without evidence.

**AI version:**
> Our revolutionary AI platform delivers groundbreaking results through cutting-edge technology.

**Human fix:**
> The platform cut Meridian's invoice processing time from 14 days to 3. That is not a pitch. That is a customer result.

**Rule:** All superlatives are banned unless followed by specific proof in the same sentence. "Revolutionary" without a number is empty marketing.

---

### Pattern 15: Closing with "In Conclusion" or Summary Paragraphs

**What it looks like:** Restating everything you just said in a tidy summary. AI does this because it was trained on essays and reports. Social content does not need conclusions.

**AI version:**
> In conclusion, AI is transforming how enterprises operate, and organizations that embrace these tools will be well-positioned for success in the years ahead.

**Human fix:**
> The companies deploying AI today will own their markets by 2028. The ones waiting will be acquired by them.

**Rule:** End with a punch, not a summary. The last sentence should be the most memorable sentence in the piece.
