## HERO Archetype

The Hero tells transformation stories. Struggle, change, result. The reader sees themselves in the journey.

---

### Hero Example 1: The Failed AI Launch (Product Launch)

**Topic:** Learning from a failed product release

I launched our AI agent to 200 beta users on a Tuesday. By Wednesday, I wanted to shut it down.

The agent hallucinated pricing data for 3 different customers. One sales rep sent a quote with a number the AI made up. The customer called. They were not happy.

I pulled the feature. The team was demoralized. Six months of work, and we could not keep it live for 24 hours.

Here is what I missed: I tested for accuracy on clean data. I never tested on the messy, incomplete data our sales team actually works with. The model worked perfectly in the lab. It failed on contact with reality.

We spent the next 3 weeks building a validation layer. Every number the AI generates gets checked against the source system before anyone sees it. Every. Single. Number.

We relaunched 4 weeks later. Zero hallucinations in 90 days. That validation layer is now our biggest competitive advantage.

The lesson is simple. Do not test AI on the data you wish you had. Test it on the data you actually have.

### Hero Example 2: The Revenue Drought (Business Challenge)

**Topic:** Surviving a revenue plateau

For 5 months, our revenue did not move. $83K MRR. Stuck.

I tried everything the playbook said. More content. More ads. More SDR outreach. We added 200 leads to the pipeline every month. Close rate: 1.2%.

The pipeline was not the problem. We were selling to the wrong people.

I pulled our last 50 closed deals and studied the pattern. Every deal over $20K had one thing in common: the buyer was a VP of Operations, not a CTO. We had been targeting CTOs for 18 months.

I rewrote our ICP that afternoon. Changed the targeting on every ad. Rewrote every email sequence. Told the SDR team to stop calling engineers and start calling operations leaders.

Month 1 after the shift: $97K MRR. Month 3: $142K MRR. Same product. Same team. Different buyer.

Most companies blame the product when revenue stalls. The product is rarely the problem. The audience is.

### Hero Example 3: The Hiring Mistake (Team Building)

**Topic:** Hiring the wrong senior leader

I hired a VP of Sales who looked perfect on paper. 15 years of enterprise experience. Closed $40M+ in career revenue. Knew every framework.

He lasted 4 months.

The problem was not his skills. He was brilliant. The problem was speed. He wanted 6-month plans, quarterly reviews, and a 12-person team before making his first call. We had 8 employees and needed revenue this quarter.

I learned something that still guides every hire I make. In a startup, the best person for the job at a big company is often the worst person for the job at yours. Enterprise experience teaches process. Startups need pace.

Now I ask one question in every interview: "Tell me about the last time you shipped something in under a week with no budget and no team." If they cannot answer, they do not get the job.

Speed is a skill. You cannot train it. You either hire it or you do not.

### Sage Example 1: The 3-Layer AI Stack (Educational)

**Topic:** How to structure an enterprise AI implementation

Every successful enterprise AI deployment I have seen follows the same 3-layer structure. Every failure skips at least one layer.

Layer 1: Data plumbing. Before you touch a model, clean your data. Connect your systems. Build the pipes. This is boring work. It is also the work that determines whether everything else succeeds or fails. Budget 40% of your time here.

Layer 2: Model selection. Not model training. Selection. In 2026, building custom models is a waste of money for 95% of use cases. Pick a foundation model. Fine-tune if you must. But do not build from scratch unless you have a PhD team and 18 months.

Layer 3: Workflow integration. The model needs to live inside your team's daily workflow. Not in a separate tool. Not in a dashboard nobody opens. Inside the tool they already use. Salesforce. Slack. HubSpot. Wherever your team works, that is where the AI goes.

Skip Layer 1 and your model hallucinates on bad data. Skip Layer 3 and nobody uses it.

The companies that get this right see results in 30 days. The ones that skip layers are still in meetings about AI 6 months later.

### Sage Example 2: The Pricing Trap (Business Framework)

**Topic:** Why most SaaS companies underprice

Here is the pricing rule I wish someone told me 5 years ago: if nobody complains about your price, it is too low.

I see the same pattern in every early-stage SaaS company. They price at $49/month because it "feels right." They add enterprise features worth $50K in value. They still charge $49.

The math never works. At $49/month you need 2,000 customers to hit $100K MRR. At $2,000/month you need 50. Getting 50 enterprise customers is hard. Getting 2,000 is 40x harder.

Three things I have learned about pricing for enterprises.

First, price on value, not on cost. If your tool saves them $500K per year, $50K per year is a bargain. They will not blink.

Second, charge before you build. If a prospect says "I would pay for that," ask them to sign a letter of intent before you build it. Real demand comes with a signature attached.

Third, raise prices every 6 months. Grandfather existing customers. Charge new customers more. If close rates stay above 15%, you are still too cheap.

Your price tells the market what you are worth. Low prices do not attract customers. They repel enterprise buyers who assume cheap means unreliable.

### Sage Example 3: The Meeting Audit (Productivity)

**Topic:** How to find 10 hours per week by cutting meetings

I tracked every meeting I attended for 30 days. 47 meetings. 38 hours. I walked out of 62% of them wondering why I was there.

Here is the audit I now run every quarter. Three questions for every recurring meeting.

Question 1: What decision did this meeting produce in the last 4 weeks? If the answer is none, cancel it. Meetings that do not produce decisions are status updates. Status updates belong in async documents.

Question 2: Do I need to be here, or do I just need the outcome? If I only need the outcome, I leave. Someone sends me a 3-line summary. Takes 30 seconds to read instead of 30 minutes to attend.

Question 3: Could this be an async message? If the meeting is one person talking for 20 minutes and 4 people listening, it is a broadcast. Broadcasts are emails. Meetings are for disagreements that need to be resolved live.

After my first audit, I cut 19 meetings. Recovered 11 hours per week. My output went up. Nobody noticed I was missing from those calls.

The best productivity hack is not a better tool. It is fewer meetings.

## SOVEREIGN Archetype

### Sovereign Example 1: The Death of SaaS Bundles (Industry Prediction)

**Topic:** Why the all-in-one SaaS model is dying

The era of the all-in-one SaaS platform is ending.

For 15 years, companies like Salesforce, HubSpot, and Adobe built empires by bundling everything into one platform. CRM plus marketing plus analytics plus support. One login. One vendor. One bill.

That model is breaking. And AI is the reason.

When AI agents can move data between systems in milliseconds, the lock-in advantage of bundled platforms disappears. You do not need everything in one place when AI can pull from everywhere instantly.

I see the next 5 years playing out like this. Best-of-breed tools win again. Companies pick the best CRM, the best marketing tool, the best analytics platform. AI orchestration sits on top and connects them all.

Salesforce knows this. That is why they spent $28B on Slack and are building an agent ecosystem. They are not selling a CRM anymore. They are selling the orchestration layer.

The winners in 2028 will not be the companies with the biggest platform. They will be the ones with the smartest agents connecting the best tools.

Build for a world where the best tool wins. Not the biggest bundle.

---

### Sovereign Example 2: The Real AI Moat (Market Analysis)

**Topic:** Why data is no longer a competitive moat

Everyone says "data is the new oil." I disagree. Data was the moat in 2020. In 2026, the moat is workflow.

Here is why data stopped being enough. Foundation models are trained on most of the public internet. Your competitor has access to the same models you do. The data advantage is shrinking every quarter as models get better at working with less.

The companies winning right now are not the ones with the most data. They are the ones who built AI into the workflow their customers cannot live without. The AI is not a feature. It is the process.

Look at what Figma did. They did not build the best design tool. They built the tool that every designer opens first every morning. Now AI features inside Figma are not optional. They are part of how designers work.

That is the real moat. Not data. Not algorithms. Workflow dependency.

If your customers can turn off your AI features and still do their job, you do not have a moat. If turning off your AI would break their daily process, you own them for the next decade.

Build the workflow. The moat follows.

### Sovereign Example 3: The Enterprise AI Reckoning (Vision Statement)

**Topic:** What happens when AI pilots become AI mandates

2026 is the year AI stops being optional for enterprises.

For 3 years, corporations ran pilots. Innovation labs tested chatbots. R&D teams filed reports nobody read. AI was a line item in the exploration budget.

That phase is over.

Three forces killed it. First, public companies must now disclose AI productivity metrics to analysts. No disclosure means you are behind. Second, boards are asking CEOs direct questions: how much did AI save us this quarter? "We are still exploring" is no longer an acceptable answer. Third, competitors who deployed AI 18 months ago are posting results that shareholders can see.

The reckoning looks like this. Companies that piloted will either deploy or admit failure. Companies that did not pilot will panic-buy and deploy badly. A small group that deployed early and iterated will pull ahead by 3-5 years of operational advantage.

There is no middle ground left. You are either deploying AI into production workflows today, or you are writing a strategy document that will be obsolete by the time it is approved.

The market does not care about your roadmap. It cares about your results. Ship or get shipped.
