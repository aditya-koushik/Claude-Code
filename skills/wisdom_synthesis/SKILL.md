# Wisdom Synthesis

## Purpose
Synthesize insights from multiple domains, frameworks, and sources into unified strategic wisdom that is greater than the sum of its parts. Goes beyond summarizing to find the connecting threads, universal principles, and non-obvious implications that emerge only when diverse perspectives are held together. Used by Bhishma as the capstone thinking skill -- invoked when multiple analyses exist but the overarching meaning has not yet crystallized.

## When to Use
- Multiple analyses (business, technical, strategic) exist but no unified picture has emerged
- The team has data and frameworks but lacks a clear narrative or direction
- Cross-domain insights need to be woven into a coherent strategic view
- Preparing executive-level communications that distill complexity into clarity
- Creating thought leadership content that connects dots others miss
- End-of-cycle reflection on what was learned and what it means

## Workflow

### Step 1: Gather Insights from Multiple Sources
Collect the key findings, conclusions, and recommendations from all relevant analyses. Sources may include: business_analysis, pattern_recognition, mechanism_analysis, first_principles_thinking, strategic_advisory, and external research. Capture each insight with its source and confidence level.

### Step 2: Find Connecting Themes
Look for threads that run across multiple sources. What do the business analysis, the technical assessment, and the competitive intelligence all point toward? Identify themes that appear in 3+ sources as high-confidence connections. Note contradictions between sources as areas requiring resolution.

### Step 3: Identify Universal Principles
Elevate connecting themes to universal principles -- truths that hold across contexts, industries, and time periods. Test each principle: Does it apply beyond the current situation? Could it inform decisions in an unrelated domain? Has it been validated across multiple historical examples?

### Step 4: Apply to Current Context
Bring the universal principles back to the specific situation at hand. What do these principles, taken together, tell us we should do? What do they warn us against? What opportunity or threat do they reveal that no single analysis surfaced on its own?

### Step 5: Produce the Synthesis
Write a synthesis that is: more than a summary (it reveals new meaning), actionable (it points toward specific decisions), and memorable (it can be communicated in a conversation). The synthesis should make the reader say "I had all these pieces but never saw how they fit together."

## Output Format
- **Source Inventory**: List of inputs with key finding from each
- **Connecting Themes**: 3-5 themes that run across multiple sources
- **Universal Principles**: 2-3 principles elevated from the themes
- **Synthesis Statement**: 3-5 sentence unified insight that integrates everything
- **Strategic Implications**: What this means for decisions, priorities, and direction
- **Contrarian Insight**: One non-obvious conclusion that challenges conventional thinking
- **Open Questions**: What the synthesis does not answer and what would be needed to resolve it

## Key Principles
- The best insights come from unexpected connections between distant domains
- Wisdom is pattern recognition across time; it requires both breadth and depth
- Synthesize, do not summarize; a synthesis reveals meaning that no single source contains
- Contradictions between sources are not errors; they are the most fertile ground for insight
- The most powerful synthesis fits on a single page; complexity is the enemy of wisdom
