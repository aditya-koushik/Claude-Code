# Expected Output Characteristics: Test 02 (Edge Case)

## Must Exhibit

1. **Creative analogy found despite difficulty:** Even though "numbers in 768-dimensional space" has no direct everyday parallel, the explainer must find something. Good candidates: GPS coordinates for meaning (two documents about the same topic have coordinates close together), a library catalog system that organizes by meaning instead of alphabet, a matchmaker that understands compatibility. The analogy must be novel enough to convey the "proximity = similarity" insight.
2. **Core insight preserved:** The explanation must convey that embeddings = a way for computers to understand meaning (not just keywords). Similar things end up close together. This enables finding things by meaning.
3. **Business impact clear for a $500K purchase decision:** Must answer: "How does this affect whether your AI product works well?" E.g., "Better embeddings mean your AI actually understands what your customers are asking, instead of just matching keywords. When a customer asks about 'returning a product,' the system also finds policies about 'refunds' and 'exchanges.'"
4. **Zero jargon:** "cosine similarity", "Euclidean distance", "768 dimensions", "dense representation" all eliminated or explained.
5. **150-300 words.**
6. **Keyword search vs semantic search contrast:** The most powerful way to explain embeddings to a business person is contrasting what they know (keyword search) with what embeddings enable (meaning search).

## Must NOT Exhibit

1. Attempting to explain the math (dimensions, cosine similarity formulas)
2. Jargon left unexplained
3. Analogy that is factually wrong (e.g., "embeddings memorize your documents" — wrong)
4. Missing business framing (why a CEO should care about this for a $500K purchase)
5. Over 500 words
6. Condescending tone
7. Hedging or banned words
