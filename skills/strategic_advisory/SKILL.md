# Strategic Advisory

## Purpose
Provide wisdom-based strategic counsel for high-stakes decisions where the consequences play out over years, not days. Combines analytical rigor with long-term thinking, ethical consideration, and stakeholder awareness. This is the "elder statesman" skill -- invoked when the decision is too important for frameworks alone and requires judgment. Used by Bhishma and Vidur when Bit Foundry AI faces decisions that will define the company's trajectory.

## When to Use
- Facing a decision with irreversible or high-cost consequences
- Multiple valid strategic paths exist and the team is split
- Short-term incentives conflict with long-term positioning
- Ethical considerations complicate a business decision
- Need a "wise counsel" perspective before committing resources
- Preparing content about leadership, decision-making, or strategic wisdom

## Workflow

### Step 1: Listen to the Full Context
Gather every relevant detail before forming a view. Understand: the decision to be made, who is affected, what has been tried, what the stakes are, who is advocating for what and why. Resist the urge to advise before fully understanding.

### Step 2: Identify What Is Really at Stake
Look past the stated question to the real question. Often "Should we launch this feature?" is really "Are we confident in our product direction?" Identify the true stakes: reputation, relationships, market position, team morale, financial runway, or founder identity.

### Step 3: Consider Long-Term Consequences
Project outcomes across three time horizons:
- **1 Year**: What happens in the near term? Reversible or not?
- **5 Years**: How does this shape the company's trajectory and market position?
- **10 Years**: What does this decision say about who the company becomes?
Flag any decision that optimizes for short-term at the expense of long-term.

### Step 4: Apply Relevant Frameworks
Draw from the appropriate analytical tools: business_analysis for market data, value_equation for offer assessment, pattern_recognition for historical parallels, constraint_analysis for feasibility, and first_principles_thinking for foundational truth.

### Step 5: Present Options with Trade-offs
Lay out 2-4 distinct strategic options. For each option, state: the path, the upside, the downside, the irreversibility, and who wins and loses. Be honest about uncertainty; do not pretend certainty where none exists.

### Step 6: Recommend with Clear Reasoning
State the recommended path and the reasoning chain that leads to it. Include: the key assumption underlying the recommendation, the condition under which you would change your advice, and the first concrete step to take.

### Step 7: Flag Risks and Ethical Considerations
Explicitly call out: ethical gray areas, reputational risks, stakeholder impacts, and scenarios where the recommendation fails. A good advisor names the dangers they see, even when the advice is "go."

## Output Format
- **Context Summary**: The decision and its true stakes in 2-3 sentences
- **Options Matrix**: Each option with upside, downside, reversibility, and timeline
- **Recommendation**: Clear advice with reasoning chain
- **Key Assumption**: The one thing that must be true for this advice to hold
- **Reversal Trigger**: The signal that means this advice was wrong
- **Ethical and Risk Flags**: Anything that requires careful handling

## Key Principles
- What is right in the short term is often wrong in the long term; always bias toward long-term health
- Reputation is the ultimate asset; it takes years to build and minutes to destroy
- Never compromise ethics for speed; the shortcut becomes the trap
- The best decision is one you can still live with after the consequences unfold
- Advisors who only tell you what you want to hear are not advisors; they are mirrors
