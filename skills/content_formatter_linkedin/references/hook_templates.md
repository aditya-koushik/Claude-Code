# LinkedIn Hook Templates

## 20 Proven Hook Templates Organized by Type

Every hook must do one job: stop the scroll. The first 2 lines get 80% of your engagement. A great hook earns the click to "See more." A weak hook buries your best writing.

---

## Type 1: Contrarian (Challenge a popular belief)

**Why it works:** People cannot resist reading something that contradicts what they believe. Contrarian hooks generate the most comments because readers feel compelled to agree or argue.

### Template 1:
> Everyone says [POPULAR BELIEF]. They are wrong.

**Example:**
> Everyone says AI will replace content teams. They are wrong.

### Template 2:
> [POPULAR ADVICE] is the worst advice in [INDUSTRY].

**Example:**
> "Move fast and break things" is the worst advice in enterprise software.

### Template 3:
> Stop [COMMON ACTION]. It is killing your [METRIC].

**Example:**
> Stop chasing product-market fit. It is killing your revenue.

### Template 4:
> The [INDUSTRY] industry has been lying to you about [TOPIC].

**Example:**
> The SaaS industry has been lying to you about churn rates.

---

## Type 2: Statistic (Lead with a specific number)

**Why it works:** Specific numbers stop the eye. They signal real data, not opinions. The more surprising the number, the stronger the hook.

### Template 5:
> [X]% of [GROUP] will [OUTCOME] by [YEAR]. Here is why.

**Example:**
> 73% of enterprises will run AI agents in production by 2027. Here is why.

### Template 6:
> We [ACTION] [METRIC] from [BAD NUMBER] to [GOOD NUMBER] in [TIME].

**Example:**
> We cut our sales cycle from 47 days to 12 in one quarter.

### Template 7:
> [NUMBER] companies. [NUMBER] [UNIT]. One pattern nobody talks about.

**Example:**
> 200 enterprise deals. $34M in pipeline. One pattern nobody talks about.

### Template 8:
> I spent [TIME/MONEY] on [THING]. Here is what actually worked.

**Example:**
> I spent $240,000 on LinkedIn ads last year. Here is what actually worked.

---

## Type 3: Story (Drop into a specific moment)

**Why it works:** Stories activate a different part of the brain than facts do. A specific time, place, or person makes readers feel like they are there. They click to find out what happened next.

### Template 9:
> Last [DAY], [PERSON/ROLE] said something that changed how I think about [TOPIC].

**Example:**
> Last Tuesday, our CTO said something that changed how I think about hiring.

### Template 10:
> [TIME PERIOD] ago, I [DID SOMETHING]. It was the best decision I ever made.

**Example:**
> Two years ago, I fired our entire sales team. It was the best decision I ever made.

### Template 11:
> I got a call from [PERSON/ROLE] at [TIME]. They said [SHORT QUOTE].

**Example:**
> I got a call from our biggest client at 11 PM. They said "We are switching to your competitor."

### Template 12:
> [PERSON/ROLE] asked me [QUESTION]. I did not have an answer.

**Example:**
> Our investor asked me one question at the board meeting. I did not have an answer.

---

## Type 4: Question (Ask something they cannot ignore)

**Why it works:** Questions create an open loop in the reader's mind. They cannot scroll past without at least thinking about the answer. Questions also drive comments because readers want to share their take.

### Template 13:
> What happens when [SCENARIO]?

**Example:**
> What happens when your SaaS vendor disappears overnight?

### Template 14:
> Why do [X]% of [GROUP] fail at [THING]?

**Example:**
> Why do 80% of AI implementations fail in the first year?

### Template 15:
> If [CONDITION], would you [ACTION]?

**Example:**
> If your biggest client left tomorrow, would your business survive?

### Template 16:
> What is the one thing [SUCCESSFUL GROUP] does that [UNSUCCESSFUL GROUP] does not?

**Example:**
> What is the one thing companies with 99.99% uptime do that everyone else skips?

---

## Type 5: Bold Claim (Make a declarative statement)

**Why it works:** Bold claims demand attention. They position you as someone with strong opinions and real experience. Weak writers hedge. Strong writers declare.

### Template 17:
> [THING] is dead. Here is what replaces it.

**Example:**
> The system of record is dead. Here is what replaces it.

### Template 18:
> The biggest threat to [INDUSTRY/ROLE] is not [EXPECTED THING]. It is [UNEXPECTED THING].

**Example:**
> The biggest threat to SaaS companies is not AI. It is their own data architecture.

### Template 19:
> In [TIME PERIOD], [PREDICTION]. Most [GROUP] are not ready.

**Example:**
> In 18 months, AI agents will handle 40% of enterprise customer support. Most support teams are not ready.

### Template 20:
> [NUMBER] years of [EXPERIENCE] taught me one thing: [INSIGHT].

**Example:**
> 12 years of selling to enterprises taught me one thing: the buyer is never the user.

---

## Hook Writing Rules

1. **First line stands alone.** It must work as a complete thought. No dependent clauses that require the second line.

2. **Maximum 15 words for the first line.** Shorter is better. The strongest hooks are 5-10 words.

3. **Blank line after the hook.** Always. This creates visual breathing room and pushes the "See more" fold after the hook, forcing a click.

4. **No hashtags in the hook.** Ever.

5. **No emojis in the hook.** They cheapen the opening.

6. **Use periods, not exclamation marks.** "The system of record is dead." reads as confident. "The system of record is dead!" reads as desperate.

7. **Specificity beats cleverness.** "We cut costs by 47%" beats "We found a game-changing way to reduce costs." Every time.

8. **Test your hook by asking: would I stop scrolling to read this?** If the answer is "maybe," rewrite it.

---

## Hook Type Selection Guide

| Content Goal | Best Hook Type | Why |
|-------------|---------------|-----|
| Drive comments | Contrarian, Question | Provokes responses |
| Build authority | Bold Claim, Statistic | Positions you as an expert |
| Build connection | Story | Creates emotional resonance |
| Drive clicks/saves | Statistic, Bold Claim | Promises valuable information |
| Educate | Question, Statistic | Opens the learning loop |
| Promote an offer | Statistic, Story | Results-first framing |
