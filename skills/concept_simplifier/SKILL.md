# Concept Simplifier

## Purpose
Takes complex technical concepts and explains them so a non-technical business leader can understand and act on them. Uses everyday analogies, three levels of explanation, and concrete examples. If a 12-year-old cannot understand the one-sentence version, it is not simple enough.

## When to Use
- When content includes technical concepts the target audience may not know
- When explaining AI, software, or data concepts to business leaders
- When Agastya or Chitrasena flags a draft as too technical
- When creating educational content for the ICP (entrepreneurs, decision-makers)

## Workflow

### Step 1: Identify the Concept
Name the exact technical concept that needs simplifying. Strip away jargon. What is the core idea underneath the technical language?

### Step 2: Find the Simplest Accurate Analogy
Map the concept to something from everyday life. "A neural network is like a team of interns. Each one learns one small task. Together they solve the big problem." The analogy must be accurate, not just catchy. A bad analogy is worse than no analogy.

### Step 3: Explain in Three Levels
- **One sentence**: A 12-year-old must understand this. "An API is a waiter. It takes your order to the kitchen and brings back your food."
- **One paragraph**: A business leader gets the full picture. Add why it matters and one real-world example.
- **One page**: A curious reader gets depth. Add how it works, what the trade-offs are, and when to use it vs. alternatives.

### Step 4: Use Concrete Examples
Replace abstract descriptions with specific scenarios. Not "AI can process data faster" but "AI reads 10,000 contracts in the time it takes a lawyer to read 3." Numbers and comparisons make concepts real.

### Step 5: Verify Simplicity
Read the one-sentence version aloud. If you stumble, simplify. Check for banned words and jargon. Target Grade 5 reading level. If any word has more than 3 syllables, ask if a simpler word works.

## Output Format
Deliver all three levels:
1. **One sentence** (max 20 words)
2. **One paragraph** (3-5 sentences)
3. **One page** (200-400 words)

Include the analogy and at least one concrete example with real numbers.

## Key Principles
- Complexity is the enemy of adoption. If they do not understand it, they will not buy it.
- Use analogies from everyday life. Kitchens, sports, traffic, hiring.
- If you cannot explain it simply, you do not understand it well enough.
- Accuracy matters. A wrong analogy is worse than a complex explanation.
- The target audience is smart but not technical. Respect their intelligence. Simplify, do not dumb down.
- Mental models: Simplicity Tax, First Principles, Value Density.
