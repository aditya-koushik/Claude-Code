# Pattern Recognition

## Purpose
Identify recurring patterns across business scenarios, markets, industries, and historical decisions. Recognizes when current situations rhyme with past events, enabling faster and better-informed decisions. Distinguishes genuine patterns from coincidental correlations. Used by Ashwatthama, Vidur, and Bhishma when the team needs historical context, cross-industry insight, or early warning signals.

## When to Use
- Encountering a business situation that "feels familiar" but the analogy is not yet clear
- Analyzing whether a market trend is a genuine shift or a temporary blip
- Looking for cross-industry lessons applicable to the current challenge
- Identifying early warning signs of failure or opportunity
- Creating content that draws non-obvious connections across domains

## Workflow

### Step 1: Collect Observations
Gather the raw data points, events, or signals to be analyzed. Include quantitative data (metrics, timelines, growth rates) and qualitative observations (market sentiment, leadership behavior, ecosystem changes). Cast a wide net; filtering comes next.

### Step 2: Categorize by Domain
Sort observations into domains: technology adoption, market dynamics, competitive behavior, organizational patterns, economic cycles, regulatory patterns, consumer behavior. Look for clusters within and across domains.

### Step 3: Look for Repetition
Search for the same pattern appearing in different contexts. Test: "Have I seen this movie before?" Compare current observations against known archetypes:
- **S-Curve Adoption**: Slow start, rapid growth, plateau
- **Innovator's Dilemma**: Incumbent ignores low-end disruption until too late
- **Winner-Take-All**: Network effects concentrate market in 1-2 players
- **Hype Cycle**: Peak of inflated expectations, trough of disillusionment, plateau of productivity
- **Founder Mode to Manager Mode**: Transition that kills or transforms companies

### Step 4: Test Pattern Validity
Challenge every identified pattern: Is this a genuine causal pattern or a coincidence? Check for: sufficient sample size, controlled variables, survivorship bias, and confirmation bias. Assign confidence level (Low/Medium/High).

### Step 5: Name and Document the Pattern
Give each validated pattern a memorable name. Document with: description, historical examples (minimum 3), triggering conditions, typical timeline, and expected outcome. A well-named pattern becomes a reusable thinking tool.

### Step 6: Apply to Current Situation
Map the identified pattern onto the current context. Identify: where we are in the pattern's lifecycle, what the pattern predicts happens next, and what actions the pattern suggests. Note where the current situation diverges from the pattern.

## Output Format
- **Observations Summary**: Key data points and signals collected
- **Patterns Identified**: Named patterns with confidence levels
- **Historical Parallels**: 2-3 examples per pattern from different industries/eras
- **Current Situation Mapping**: Where we are in the pattern, what comes next
- **Actionable Implications**: What the pattern suggests we should do now
- **Pattern Limitations**: Where the analogy breaks down and why

## Key Principles
- History does not repeat, but it rhymes; look for the rhyme, not the exact match
- Successful patterns transfer across industries; human behavior is the constant
- Beware of false patterns: correlation does not equal causation, and small samples lie
- The most valuable patterns are non-obvious ones that connect distant domains
- A pattern recognized early is a strategic advantage; recognized late is a history lesson
